## 3. HOW IT WORKS

*5 steps. Clear, sequential, concrete. Must feel like a real system, not a marketing claim.*

---

### Step-by-Step: The Decision Layer in Practice

---

**Step 1 — You supply context, not just a prompt**

You enter what the copy needs to do: the product or service, the audience, the goal, the format, and any specific requirements. If you have a website, CopyZap extracts the relevant context automatically. If you have a brand voice profile, it applies it without being asked. The brief is built from structured inputs — not a single text box.

*Why this matters: the quality of the evaluation downstream depends on the quality of the context going in. Structured input produces context-aware scoring. A prompt box does not.*

---

**Step 2 — CopyZap generates multiple versions**

The system produces two or more distinct versions of the copy — each taking a different angle, emphasis, or approach. This is not surface variation. It is substantive: one version might lead with the outcome, another with the problem, another with the proof. The variation is strategic.

*Why this matters: you can only run a comparison if you have genuinely different things to compare. Generating one version and asking if it is good is a weaker question than generating three and asking which is best.*

---

**Step 3 — Every version is automatically scored**

Each version is evaluated across clarity, persuasion, trust, and conversion signals. The moment output is generated, the scoring engine evaluates each version independently across these dimensions. Scores are weighted for the specific use case — an ad is not scored the same way as a landing page. Scores are not cosmetic. They are calculated against concrete criteria.

*Why this matters: the score tells you something about the output that reading it alone cannot. You can read a piece of copy and think it sounds good. The score tells you whether it is constructed well across all the dimensions that actually drive performance.*

---

**Step 4 — All versions are compared in a structured framework**

The comparison layer places all versions side by side in a common framework. Score deltas are calculated — not just "Version A scored 84," but "Version A scored 11 points higher than Version B on persuasion." The framework surfaces where each version leads, where it falls behind, and what the total margin is.

*Why this matters: a score in isolation is less meaningful than a score in context. Knowing one version scored 84 is useful. Knowing it scored 84 and outperformed the next best version on the three dimensions most relevant to your goal is a decision.*

---

**Step 5 — A winner is declared with a written rationale**

The system identifies the highest-performing version and outputs a strategic recommendation: which version won, why it won, what specific strengths drove its score, and what to consider before publishing. The recommendation is not a confidence percentage. It is a structured explanation you can act on — or hand to a client.

*Why this matters: a number without explanation is not a decision. The written rationale is what transforms a score into a publishing recommendation with documented justification.*

## 4. SCORING EXPLANATION

*Demystify the scoring. Name the dimensions. Explain why it is reliable. Kill the "is this just a random number?" objection.*

---

### What Gets Scored

Each version is evaluated across clarity, persuasion, trust, and conversion signals — plus additional dimensions that activate based on your stated goal:

| Dimension | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| **Clarity** | Can the reader understand the core message without re-reading? Is the structure logical? Does the language serve the point? |
| **Persuasion** | Does the copy address why the reader should care? Does it create desire, urgency, or motivation to act? |
| **Trust** | Does the copy feel credible? Does it avoid overclaiming? Does it provide reasons to believe? |
| **Audience Fit** | Is the language, tone, and reference frame appropriate for the stated audience? |
| **Call-to-Action Quality** | Is the next step clear? Is the CTA specific and frictionless? |
| **Structural Quality** | Is the copy appropriately formatted for the stated format? Does the structure serve the objective? |
| **SEO Performance** | Are target keywords present at appropriate density? Are SEO structural signals in place? (Scored when SEO is the objective) |
| **GEO Readiness** | Is the copy structured to be surfaced and cited by AI search responses? Are AI-discoverability signals present? |

---

### How Scores Are Weighted

Weights shift based on use case.

An ad copy evaluation weights persuasion and CTA quality most heavily — structural compliance matters less. A landing page evaluation weights clarity, trust, and conversion intent. An SEO blog post evaluation weights SEO and structural quality alongside readability. The score you get reflects what actually matters for how the copy will be used.

This means scores are context-aware, not generic. An 82 on an ad is not the same calculation as an 82 on a case study.

---

### Why This Is More Reliable Than Intuition

Three reasons:

**1. It is consistent.** The same criteria are applied to every version, every time. Human editorial judgment fluctuates with mood, context, and who is in the room. The scoring framework does not.

**2. It is specific.** A score is accompanied by evidence — excerpts from the actual copy, specific feedback on what is driving the score up or down. "The opening hook lacks urgency relative to the offer" is a different quality of feedback than "it just does not feel right."

**3. It is comparative.** The score only becomes a decision tool when you can compare two or more versions against the same standard. Human readers rarely hold two versions of a 400-word landing page in their head simultaneously with equal neutrality. The scoring framework does this automatically and calculates the delta.

---

### What Scores Cannot Do

Scores are evaluation tools. They do not replace judgment — they inform it.

A version that scores 91 but contains a claim that is wrong for your specific context still requires human review. A version that scores 77 but uses a specific phrase your brand has adopted for cultural reasons may still be the right choice. The score is the most reliable input available for the decision. It is not the only input.

CopyZap is designed to be used with the understanding that evidence improves decisions — it does not automate them.

## 5. KEY DIFFERENTIATION BLOCK

*Direct, specific, no diplomacy. What they do. What CopyZap does instead.*

---

### What Other Tools Do

Every major AI writing platform — ChatGPT, Jasper, Copy.ai, and every tool built on the same pattern — operates the same way:

1. You describe what you want
2. The tool generates text
3. You read the text
4. You decide if it is good enough
5. You publish, revise, or try again

Step 4 is entirely on you. The tool is done. It generated. You evaluate. You decide. If your judgment is good, the output is good. If your judgment is uncertain — as it is for most users most of the time — the output is whatever it is.

No AI writing tool currently in the market generates copy and then evaluates the copy it generated. None of them calculate quality scores. None of them compare versions in a structured framework. None of them produce a winner declaration with a written rationale. None of them close the evaluation loop.

They were built to generate. Not to evaluate. The evaluation step was left to you.

---

### What CopyZap Does Instead

CopyZap treats evaluation as a first-class function — not an optional feature, not a bolt-on.

- Every output is scored automatically. You do not request a score. It happens.
- Every session is built for comparison from the start. The comparison framework is not a separate mode.
- The winner is declared by the system. You do not have to guess which version is best.
- The rationale is written. You do not have to interpret a number on your own.
- The evidence is anchored to the actual copy — specific phrases, specific sections, specific weaknesses.

The generation capability exists to serve the evaluation capability. CopyZap generates so it has something to evaluate. The output you receive is not a draft to work from — it is the result of a scored, compared, evidence-backed evaluation process.

That is the difference. It is not a user interface difference. It is a fundamental difference in what the system is designed to do.

---

### The Single Most Important Contrast

> **Other tools hand you the output and step back.**
> **CopyZap tells you which output to use and why.**

That is the entire decision layer in one sentence.

## 6. MINI EXAMPLE

*Two real-feeling versions. One wins. Explanation names exactly why. Must feel like a real system output, not a made-up demo.*

---

### Scenario:
SaaS product. Landing page hero copy. Goal: increase trial signups. Audience: small business owners who have already tried a competitor and been disappointed.

---

### Version A — Score: 84

> Stop managing projects across six different tools.
>
> [Product Name] brings your tasks, team, and timelines into one place — so you stop losing things in Slack threads and start actually shipping.
>
> Built for teams of 5 to 50. Set up in under 10 minutes.
>
> Start your free trial — no card required.

---

### Version B — Score: 67

> The all-in-one project management solution for growing teams.
>
> Streamline your workflows, increase productivity, and collaborate effectively with [Product Name]'s powerful suite of tools designed to help modern businesses succeed.
>
> Sign up today.

---

### Decision Layer Output:

**Winner: Version A**
**Margin: +17 points**
**Decision Badge: Best Conversion**

**Why Version A won:**

*Clarity (A: 91 / B: 58):*
Version A opens with a specific, recognizable problem ("six different tools") rather than a category claim. The reader knows immediately what this product is for. Version B opens with a generic category label that communicates nothing the reader does not already assume.

*Persuasion (A: 86 / B: 61):*
Version A names the actual pain ("losing things in Slack threads") and connects it to a concrete outcome ("start actually shipping"). Version B uses abstracted benefit language ("streamline workflows," "increase productivity") that audiences have been trained to ignore because it appears in every SaaS product description.

*Trust (A: 79 / B: 72):*
Both versions are comparable on trust. Version A benefits slightly from specificity ("teams of 5 to 50," "10 minutes") which signals that the claims are grounded rather than aspirational.

*CTA Quality (A: 88 / B: 54):*
Version A's CTA names an outcome ("Start your free trial") and removes the primary barrier ("no card required") in the same line. Version B's CTA ("Sign up today") is instruction-only with no friction reduction and no value reinforcement.

**Recommended action:** Publish Version A. Consider testing whether replacing "six different tools" with a specific named competitor (if brand guidelines permit) increases conversion further.

---

### What This Example Shows:

- Scoring is dimensional, not a single arbitrary number
- The rationale references the actual copy — not generic copywriting advice
- The recommendation is specific and actionable
- The margin tells you how confident the system is in the recommendation

## STRATEGIC BRIEF (Before Copy)

Use cases are the most underestimated section on most product websites. Most teams write them as vague personas ("marketers who want better results") and wrap them in feature language. That does not work.

What works is specificity of situation. Not who the person is — what they are doing on Tuesday afternoon and what is making it harder than it should be.

Each use case in this section follows the same structure: the situation (what is actually happening), the problem (what the situation costs them), the change CopyZap introduces, and the outcome. Every section ends with a single-sentence summary — the version a visitor would tell a colleague.

Five use cases are defined. Each is distinct enough that a visitor landing on the wrong one will keep reading until they find the right one.

## USE CASE 1 — THE FOUNDER

### Title:
**You know your product. You do not know how to write about it.**

---

### Quick Summary Line:
For founders writing their own marketing copy, CopyZap turns business knowledge into publish-ready copy — without requiring copywriting skills or trial-and-error prompt engineering.

---

### Situation:

You built the product. You know every detail of how it works and why it matters. But when you sit down to write the homepage, the ad copy, or the launch email, it does not come out right.

You either write it yourself — spending two hours on a paragraph that still feels off — or you paste a vague brief into an AI tool and get text that sounds like every other AI-generated paragraph on the internet. Technically correct. Completely generic. Not yours.

You have revised the homepage three times. The headline still does not feel like it captures what you actually do. The About page reads like a template. You are not sure if the problem is you, the tools, or both.

---

### Problem:

AI writing tools require you to already know how to structure a brief, how to write a good prompt, and how to evaluate the output critically. They produce text when asked. Whether the text is good is entirely on you to determine.

Founders are expert in their business. They are rarely expert in persuasion architecture, tone calibration, or conversion copy structure. The result: you are using a tool that expects skills you do not have, and producing copy that reflects that gap.

The deeper problem: you cannot tell if the copy you produced is strong or just passable. You publish it because the deadline passed, not because you are confident in it.

---

### How CopyZap Helps:

The Quick Setup Wizard walks you through supplying what you already know — your audience, your core message, what problem you solve, what makes you different. No prompt engineering. No copywriting vocabulary required. You answer questions about your business. The system handles the copy.

Every piece of generated copy is scored across clarity, persuasion strength, trust signals, and conversion intent. You do not have to guess if it is good. The score tells you where it stands and what the specific weaknesses are.

When multiple versions are generated, the comparison framework ranks them and identifies the strongest — so the decision of which version to use is not left to instinct.

---

### Outcome:

You stop spending hours second-guessing copy you were never trained to write. You produce publish-ready outputs that have been evaluated against structured criteria, not just your gut feeling at 11pm. The homepage gets done. The launch email goes out. You move on.

**Confidence gained:** You are no longer publishing copy and hoping for the best. You have evidence that what you published was the best version available from your inputs.

## USE CASE 2 — THE IN-HOUSE MARKETER

### Title:
**You are producing more content than ever. You are not more confident in any of it.**

---

### Quick Summary Line:
For in-house marketers managing high volume across multiple channels, CopyZap replaces gut-based selection with a structured evaluation framework that makes every publishing decision defensible.

---

### Situation:

You run copy for the company across ads, landing pages, email sequences, and social. You have an AI tool — possibly two. Production speed is not your problem. You are outputting more content than ever.

But every week you face the same unresolved question: is this actually good? You read the options, you pick the one that feels strongest, and you send it. Sometimes it works. Sometimes it does not. You cannot always explain the difference.

When a campaign underperforms, the post-mortem is inconclusive. The audience targeting seems fine. The bid strategy seems fine. The copy — no one can say for certain. You move on and do the same thing next week.

The revision process is its own problem. Stakeholders push back on copy for reasons that are hard to argue against because they are subjective. Someone in the room prefers a different headline. The debate is about taste. It takes longer than it should and the outcome is determined by seniority, not evidence.

---

### Problem:

You are producing at speed but selecting blind. The tools that generate your content have no mechanism for telling you which output is better, why it is better, or whether either option clears a quality bar worth publishing.

The result is a volume-confidence mismatch: output is high, confidence is low, and the process that was supposed to improve your marketing has just increased the amount of material that goes through the same flawed selection process.

The stakeholder review problem compounds this. Without an objective quality layer, every review session is a negotiation of preferences. That costs time and rarely improves the copy.

---

### How CopyZap Helps:

Every generation produces scored outputs — not just text. The multi-dimensional score evaluates each version across the criteria that matter for the specific format: conversion intent for ads, trust signals for landing pages, clarity and CTA for email.

When stakeholders push back, you have a score, a ranked comparison, and deep analysis with specific named strengths and weaknesses. The conversation shifts from "I prefer version B" to "version B scores 14 points higher on persuasion and the analysis explains exactly why."

Brand voice profiles ensure that copy produced across different campaigns applies a consistent standard — not a different interpretation each time a different person touches the brief.

---

### Outcome:

Publishing decisions have evidence behind them. Stakeholder review sessions have a structured basis for resolution. The copy that goes live is the version that scored highest, not the version that was approved by the most senior person in the room.

**Speed gained:** The selection decision — which used to take a full review cycle — is resolved at generation time.

**Confidence gained:** You can answer "why this version" with something more than instinct.

## USE CASE 3 — THE AGENCY COPYWRITER

### Title:
**Your clients want revisions. They cannot tell you what is wrong. You cannot prove what is right.**

---

### Quick Summary Line:
For agency copywriters managing client review cycles, CopyZap replaces subjective creative debate with scored, evidence-backed copy decisions that clients can understand and approve faster.

---

### Situation:

You deliver copy. The client reads it. They say they want it to feel "punchier" or "more professional" or "a bit more like us." You revise. They review again. They are still not sure. Round three begins.

This is not unusual. It is the standard pattern of almost every agency copy engagement. The client cannot articulate what they want changed because they do not have the vocabulary. You cannot fully defend your choices because the brief was interpreted, not measured. The loop continues until someone runs out of patience.

You are also fielding briefs that are incomplete. The client sends three paragraphs of context and expects a landing page that captures their full brand positioning. You fill in the gaps from what you can infer. You deliver something solid. They tell you it does not quite sound like them.

---

### Problem:

The revision cycle is a symptom of a missing quality standard. There is no shared framework between you and the client for what "good" looks like. Both sides are evaluating subjectively. The gap between your assessment and theirs has no mechanism for resolution.

The brief problem compounds the cycle. Incomplete briefs produce copy that is technically competent but contextually incomplete. The client's response — "it's not quite us" — is accurate but not actionable. You have no way to extract the missing context systematically on the first pass.

---

### How CopyZap Helps:

The structured input system forces brief completeness before generation begins. Audience definition, tone profile, key message, pain points, CTA, funnel stage — all required, all captured. When a client provides three paragraphs, the wizard surfaces the gaps. Briefs do not leave the intake process incomplete.

Brand voice is extracted directly from the client's website and encoded as a reusable profile. Every generation applies that encoded voice — not your interpretation of it, not the client's vague instruction. The actual structural patterns of how they write: sentence rhythm, POV, formality, vocabulary complexity.

When you deliver copy with scores and a comparison table, the conversation changes. You are not defending creative instincts. You are presenting evaluated outputs with documented reasoning. The client can disagree with a score — that is fine — but the disagreement now has a basis, which makes it resolvable.

---

### Outcome:

First-pass approval rates increase because briefs are complete and voice is applied precisely, not estimated. Revision cycles shorten because there is an objective layer mediating the creative disagreement. You spend less time defending work and more time producing it.

**Confidence gained:** You can tell a client which version is stronger and produce evidence for that claim immediately.

**Client relationship gained:** The delivery is more professional. The copy arrives with scoring, comparison, and documented reasoning — not just a Word document in an email.

## USE CASE 4 — THE PERFORMANCE MARKETER

### Title:
**You are running the tests. You are not running them on the right versions.**

---

### Quick Summary Line:
For performance marketers running paid campaigns, CopyZap ensures the copy variants entering your A/B tests are the strongest available — evaluated before launch, not after spend.

---

### Situation:

You run paid acquisition. You know that copy is a primary performance variable — headline, primary text, CTA. You test systematically: variant A versus variant B, let the data decide.

The problem is upstream of the test. The variants you are testing were selected the same way everyone selects copy: someone wrote them, someone approved them, and they went into the test. You tested two options. You do not know if either of them was actually strong.

You have had campaigns where both variants underperformed. You increased the budget, refined the targeting, tested new audiences. The copy stayed the same because you had already decided it was fine. It was not fine. You did not have a mechanism to know.

You also write a lot of copy. Volume is necessary in performance marketing. Ad creative fatigue is real. You need new variants constantly. The production rate is manageable. The quality evaluation rate is not — there are not enough hours to deeply assess every creative before it runs.

---

### Problem:

Your testing methodology is rigorous. Your variant selection process is not. The variants entering your tests were chosen by instinct, not scored against the criteria that actually drive performance: specificity of offer, clarity of CTA, relevance of pain point to audience, persuasion architecture.

This means you are spending media budget to discover whether two unverified copy variants perform differently — when you could know before launch which one is the stronger candidate, and test that version against a genuinely different direction rather than two versions of the same instinct.

---

### How CopyZap Helps:

Every copy variant is scored before it leaves the system. Persuasion strength, clarity, CTA specificity, conversion intent — evaluated at generation time, not after the campaign has run.

The comparison framework tells you which variant is the stronger candidate before it enters your test. You are not eliminating testing — you are raising the quality floor of what enters the test. The variant that was going to drag down your results gets replaced before it costs you.

Variant generation produces multiple distinct creative directions in a single session. You are not tweaking the same line ten times. You are producing genuinely different angles — benefit-led, fear-led, proof-led, urgency-led — and selecting the strongest for test entry.

For high-volume production, saved templates and workflow sequences mean the brief setup time per ad set drops from minutes to seconds. Production scales without quality degrading.

---

### Outcome:

The copy entering your tests has been evaluated before launch. Your test results tell you which direction wins — not which unscored variant happened to perform less badly than the other.

**Performance gained:** You stop wasting test cycles on copy that was never strong. The variants that run are the best available before budget touches them.

**Speed gained:** High-volume production with consistent quality evaluation — not a bottleneck, not a batch process you skip under deadline pressure.

## USE CASE 5 — THE FREELANCE COPYWRITER

### Title:
**You deliver copy. You want to deliver copy you can stand behind.**

---

### Quick Summary Line:
For freelance copywriters managing multiple clients and tight turnarounds, CopyZap provides a systematic quality layer that makes every delivery more defensible and every client relationship more sustainable.

---

### Situation:

You write for multiple clients. Each has different voice, different audience, different standards. You are good at your job. The volume is the problem.

When you have three projects in the same week, the third one gets less attention than the first. Not because you care less — because you have less time. You deliver it. You think it is probably fine. You send it and wait for the reply.

Some clients review your copy with detailed feedback. Most say "looks good" and publish it. You never know if it was actually good, or if they just did not have the bandwidth to review it properly. Both scenarios feel the same from your side of the inbox.

The clients who give detailed feedback are valuable but difficult. They have opinions about copy they cannot always articulate clearly. You revise based on notes that feel contradictory. You produce a third draft that satisfies no one fully. The engagement ends with both sides mildly frustrated.

---

### Problem:

You are evaluating your own work under time pressure with no external quality benchmark. Your standard varies with your energy level, your familiarity with the client, and how much time you have left. That variability is invisible to you but visible to clients over time.

The client relationship problem is a downstream version of the same issue: without a structured quality standard, revision discussions have no resolution mechanism. The client's opinion and your opinion carry the same evidential weight — which is none. Whoever has more persistence wins.

---

### How CopyZap Helps:

Brand voice profiles for each client mean you are not re-interpreting their voice on every project. You extract it once from their website, name it, and apply it on every generation. The third project of the week applies exactly the same voice precision as the first one.

Every deliverable is scored. You know before you send it whether it clears a quality threshold — not based on how you feel about it at 10pm, but based on a structured evaluation against the criteria that matter for how it will be used.

When a client responds with vague feedback, you have a score and a deep analysis to reference. You know which specific dimension their feedback is targeting, and you can produce an improved version that addresses that dimension directly rather than guessing.

Saved templates and client profiles mean the setup time per project shrinks with every project you complete. The second project for a returning client is dramatically faster than the first — and the quality is higher because the context is already encoded.

---

### Outcome:

Every delivery is backed by evaluation, not hope. Client relationships are more sustainable because revision discussions have a structured basis. Your rate improves because your delivery quality is consistent and documentable.

**Confidence gained:** You send the copy knowing it was evaluated — not just written and shipped.

**Business gained:** Consistent quality across clients, even under volume. The clients who were mildly frustrated stop being mildly frustrated. The ones who were happy stay happy.

## EDITORIAL NOTES

**On use case selection:**
Five use cases were chosen because they are distinct enough to feel personally specific to each audience, while sharing a common underlying problem (evaluation gap) that reinforces the core positioning. A visitor who reads all five should finish with a stronger conviction that the problem is real — because they see it manifesting differently across five different situations.

**On the tone:**
Each situation description was written to feel mildly uncomfortable — not because the goal is to make visitors feel bad, but because recognition requires honesty. "You send it and wait for the reply" is the kind of line that either resonates immediately (because the visitor has been there) or passes cleanly (because they haven't). No one who has not experienced it will object to it. Everyone who has will stop and re-read it.

**On the quick summary lines:**
These are designed for two uses: the one-line version that appears in a card sub-header or navigation tooltip, and the internal brief line that describes who the use case is for. They should be treated as copy, not metadata — they are the sentence a visitor uses to describe CopyZap to a colleague.

**On layout recommendation:**
The card format works best with a horizontal scroll or tab-based navigation on desktop — so visitors can move quickly to the card that matches their situation. On mobile, a vertical stack with clear headers is sufficient. The section headline ("Find your situation") should encourage scanning rather than sequential reading. This is not a section visitors read start to finish — it is a section they navigate to the right position within.
